# Miller Environmental Corporation: A Research Report

## TL;DR

- Miller Environmental Corporation (MEC) is Manitoba's only fully licensed hazardous-waste treatment, storage and disposal operator — and, per President Paul Bauer's February 2026 remarks, one of only two such facilities in Canada. Its flagship Vaughn Bullough Environmental Centre (VBEC) sits near St. Jean Baptiste, Manitoba, with a Winnipeg service centre at 1803 Hekla Avenue.
- The company is not a conventional private startup. Its current Miller-operated form became effective **January 1, 1996** as a 50/50 public-private restructuring of Manitoba's existing Crown hazardous-waste system — but its institutional lineage reaches back to the province's 1982 program work and the 1986 statutory creation of the Manitoba Hazardous Waste Management Corporation (MHWMC).
- Operations are led by President Paul Bauer (since 2016; publicly named President by February 2026), with Blair McArthur signing as President & CEO at the parent/holding-company level. Long-time General Manager Vaughn Bullough — for whom the treatment facility was renamed in August 2022 — built the operation from 1997 onward.
- MEC is ISO 9001/14001/45001 certified, operates under Manitoba Licence No. 58 HW S2 RRRRR, and has no publicly reported environmental orders, fines, or community-opposition controversies in the searched public record — though it operates in a reputationally sensitive industry with ongoing licence-modernization filings (most recently a May 2026 leachate-pond repair plan).

## Executive summary

Miller Environmental is best understood as a **hybrid public-private hazardous-waste operator whose current Miller-run form dates to January 1, 1996**, layered on an operating lineage that reaches back to Manitoba's hazardous-waste program of the 1980s. Public Manitoba records show that, effective January 1, 1996, the provincial corporation entered into agreements with Miller Waste Systems (a division of Miller Paving Limited) and Miller Environmental Corporation for continued operation of Manitoba's hazardous-waste management system. A Manitoba news release on January 10, 1996 described the deal as a 50/50 public-private arrangement; later audited Manitoba financial statements describe the public side as retaining 50% of the common shares and all Class A special preferred shares while keeping title to the land and leasing it to Miller.

That ownership structure is the single most important analytical fact about the company. It means MEC emerged from a public-private restructuring rather than a greenfield founding by named entrepreneurs. On the private side, the public face of the 1996 transaction was Leo McArthur, then president and CEO of the Miller Group; on the public side, the transaction was driven by the Province of Manitoba through the MHWMC. The structure remains in place today: Manitoba retains the land and leases it to MEC, which operates the business and pays rent and dividends to the Crown shareholder.

The operating footprint is concentrated in Manitoba across two sites: the Winnipeg Service Centre at 1803 Hekla Avenue (customer service, field services, licensed PCB storage, inbound/outbound coordination) and the core treatment asset, the Vaughn Bullough Environmental Centre near St. Jean Baptiste — roughly 70 km south of Winnipeg on a 64-hectare footprint. MEC positions itself as Manitoba's integrated "cradle-to-grave" hazardous-waste manager, says it manages most waste in-house, and markets services across Manitoba, Northwestern Ontario, and surrounding areas while accepting waste from across Canada and the United States.

Public evidence of growth and modernization is strongest in the Manitoba environmental registry (File 3440.20), which records a long sequence of notices of alteration and approvals — new containment/repository cells, paint-storage authorization, leachate-management work, a leachate irrigation pilot, Processing Cell 5, a solvent-recovery approval, licence modernization, and a 2026 leachate-pond repair plan. These show a company that has continuously expanded, reconfigured, and re-permitted its infrastructure, especially since 2013. No public evidence was found of major acquisitions, a public listing, venture funding, or disclosed company revenue; the only hard financial data are indirect public-side figures from Manitoba's audited statements.

## Corporate identity, ownership and structure

MEC is a 50/50 joint venture between the Province of Manitoba — via the Manitoba Hazardous Waste Management Corporation, a Crown entity created under The Manitoba Hazardous Waste Management Corporation Act (C.C.S.M. c. H15) — and the privately held Miller Group of Markham, Ontario, operating in Manitoba through its Miller Waste Systems / White Owl Group affiliates. The Miller Group itself became a Colas Canada subsidiary on February 28, 2018, when Colas Canada completed its acquisition of 100% of the shares of the Miller McAsphalt Group for CAN $913 million; MEC sits below that parent through the Miller Waste Systems / White Owl chain.

**Founding narrative.** The cleanest description is that the current company emerged out of Manitoba's hazardous-waste policy apparatus rather than from a normal entrepreneurial launch. The historical chain runs from the province's 1982 system-development work, to passage of The Manitoba Hazardous Waste Management Corporation Act in 1986, to licensing of the St. Jean Baptiste facility in 1993, and finally to the 1995–96 public-private restructuring under which the Miller side took over operations. The key primary document is Manitoba's audited statement for the MHWMC: effective January 1, 1996, the provincial corporation entered agreements with the Miller entities that transferred certain assets and liabilities to Miller in exchange for 50% of the common shares and all Class A special preferred shares, while the province retained title to the land and leased it to Miller for an indefinite term tied to continued operation. LinkedIn lists the company as founded in 1996 and "privately held," consistent with the post-restructuring operating entity.

**Ownership ambiguity.** A 1997 Commission for Environmental Cooperation country report said the facility was "sold in 1996" to Miller, "although the province retains 49 percent ownership." Later Manitoba audited statements frame the public side's interest as 50% of common shares plus all Class A special preferred shares. The practical conclusion: the public side retained a substantial ongoing stake; the exact percentage depends on whether one counts common equity only or all share classes. The most rigorous reading of the brand architecture is that MEC is operationally part of the Miller family of businesses (Miller Waste presents "Miller Environmental" as its hazardous-waste offering; CHWMEG lists the St. Jean Baptiste owner as "The Miller Group") but legally still tied to the legacy provincial joint-venture structure.

**"Miller Environmental" naming confusion.** Two unrelated U.S. firms share the name and are a common source of confusion:
- **Miller Environmental Group Inc. (MEG), Calverton, NY** — a U.S. environmental-response company founded by Jim Miller Sr. in 1971 (Exxon Valdez responder, U.S. Coast Guard OSRO). Owned by Coalesce Capital since September 10, 2024 (acquired from GenNx360 Capital Partners). No relationship to the Canadian company.
- **Miller Environmental, Inc., Anaheim, CA** — a separate 1999-founded California demolition/abatement contractor. Not related to the Canadian company.

## Facilities and footprint

- **Vaughn Bullough Environmental Centre (VBEC)** — Hwy 14 & 75, NE 2-3-1 EPM, RM of Montcalm, near St. Jean Baptiste, MB R0G 2B0. About 70 km south of Winnipeg on a 64-hectare (~160-acre) footprint. Includes an engineered-lined landfill (repository), clay-base-lined processing cells, a decontamination facility, a hydro-vac slurry receiving area, process buildings for organic and inorganic waste treatment, batch water treatment, and a plastics shredding/recycling facility. MEC says it treats or disposes of more hazardous-waste types than any other Manitoba company.
- **Winnipeg Service Centre** — 1803 Hekla Avenue, Winnipeg, MB R2R 0K3. Hub for customer-service technicians, project/field services, and licensed PCB storage; coordinates inbound/outbound waste shipments. The public household-hazardous-waste (HHW) drop-off at this address is permanently closed; the City of Winnipeg now manages HHW through its 4R Depots.

Historically, the Winnipeg location was at **65 Trottier Bay** (per Manitoba HHW announcements in 2000 and 2004, and still used as the mailing address on a 2013 expansion cover letter). *(One earlier MEC summary cited "55 Trottier Bay"; the better-sourced address is 65 Trottier Bay.)* Miller applied in October 2006 to build and operate the Hekla Avenue collection facility, documented as a new collection facility in 2007, indicating the transition to the current base was gradual rather than instantaneous. No public evidence was found of foreign branches or international offices; the best interpretation is regional facilities with broader sourcing and project reach rather than a North-America-wide branch network.

## Services and business model

MEC is a "cradle-to-grave" hazardous-waste manager. Public service lines include: customer waste collection; 24/7 emergency response (spill containment and cleanup); environmental remediation (contaminated soil, brownfields, fire-damaged sites, UST excavation); industrial cleaning; industrial waste treatment (organic, inorganic, sludges, contaminated soils, aqueous and liquid-organic streams); project management; research & development; specialty recycling (solvent reclamation, precious-metal recovery, plastics shredding/washing); specialty processes such as inorganic-oxidizer disposal; product stewardship; chemical and container supply; and vacuum-truck services. The company holds a dedicated PCB-storage licence at the Winnipeg transfer centre and processes more than one million litres of household hazardous waste annually from Manitoba and Ontario. It says most waste is managed in-house rather than outsourced downstream — per Bauer in February 2026, "96 per cent is managed in-house and four per cent gets sent out."

## Notable projects (company case studies)

- **Decommissioned grain elevator — Winnipeg.** Remediation and demolition of a contaminated-registry grain elevator with widespread arsenic contamination, a deteriorating water-filled basement, and undocumented substructures. MEC chose full excavation/transport/treatment over unproven in-situ arsenic stabilization, coordinating with Manitoba Hydro, the City of Winnipeg Water & Waste Department, and the Winnipeg Fire Department, with contaminated soil sent to VBEC. *(The case study names no completion year.)*
- **Highway 16 diesel spill (November 15, 2024).** A saddle-tank rupture on a heavy truck released over 500 L of diesel into a roadside ditch near a CPKC railway line and a buried Bell MTS fibre cable. MEC removed ~28,000 L of diesel-contaminated water from a 45 m × 9 m area using hydro-vac soft-dig excavation, with PID monitoring, CCME compliance, and a Remedial Action Plan filed with the province. Closed in 60 days for client Manitoba Public Insurance.
- **Steinbach strip-mall fire-site hazard removal.** Post-fire asbestos abatement at a strip mall adjacent to residential housing and a children's playground; a provincial stop-work order was issued before MEC's revised abatement plan was approved. Closed in under 20 weeks, with asbestos transported in lined 80-yard bins and steel recycled. *(Undated in the source.)*
- **Brandon power-facility lime removal (March 2023).** Following a lime-silo rupture at a Brandon, MB natural-gas power plant, lime powder spread across four of five floors with exothermic-reaction and respiratory hazards. MEC used HEPA-vacuum systems under negative-pressure containment, sealing lime in IBC containers, with third-party industrial hygienist Pinchin Ltd. providing oversight. No safety incidents reported.

## Clients and market position

Client and sector profile spans industrial manufacturing, aerospace, transportation, biotech, agriculture, mining, oil & gas, chemical distribution, utilities, the rail industry, Crown insurers (e.g., MPI), municipal HHW programs (most Manitoba municipalities and select Ontario contracts), and federal/provincial agencies. The company serves residential, commercial, and industrial clients, from large manufacturers to households.

Federal procurement has been part of the revenue mix for years: CanadaBuys shows a federal Hazardous Waste Disposal Services contract awarded February 28, 2017, amended through April 8, 2026, expiring February 28, 2027, plus a Lead Removal Services contract history tied to a March 7, 2023 award. The crown-anchored, privately-operated structure underpins MEC's market position as the only fully licensed hazardous-waste treatment and disposal facility of its kind in Manitoba and one of only two in Canada per Bauer's February 2026 remarks.

## Certifications, licences and regulatory posture

MEC operates an Integrated Management System certified to **ISO 9001:2015 (Quality), ISO 14001:2015 (Environmental), and ISO 45001:2018 (Occupational Health & Safety)**, and displays an MHCA COR 2023 certification. A behaviour-based safety management system was implemented in April 2018, and Incident Command System (ICS) training was rolled out company-wide in 2021.

Key Manitoba regulatory licences: the VBEC Processing Facility Operating Licence **No. 58 HW S2 RRRRR** under the Dangerous Goods Handling & Transportation Act (evolved from No. 58 HW S2 RRRR, posted November 2015, as alterations accumulated); the 1803 Hekla Ave. Operating Licence 211 HW RR; and Hazardous Waste Transport Licence **MBC00202**. Federal hazardous-waste consignor/consignee registrations are MBG 02410 and MBR 01829. MEC is also a profiled facility in the CHWMEG audit program used by Fortune-500 generators to vet disposal vendors.

The May 2, 2025 Notice of Alteration approval (Licence 58 HW S2 RRRRR) reflects a constructive, ongoing relationship with Manitoba's Environmental Approvals Branch: MEC files modernization requests, the Branch responds with conditions, and a public Citizens Liaison Committee reviews proposals. The solvent-recovery unit (an Almatec AC-150 system in Processing Building PB1) is the most significant recent operational change, shifting MEC up the waste hierarchy from fuel-blending to true material recovery and enabling in-province recycling of roughly 4.5 million litres of contaminated solvent received annually (previously fuel-blended and shipped to U.S. cement kilns).

## Compliance record and controversies

No public enforcement orders, fines, prosecutions, or court judgments specific to MEC were located in searches of Manitoba's environmental public registry, gov.mb.ca news, or major Manitoba media (Winnipeg Free Press, CBC, Winnipeg Sun, La Liberté). The 1995 Trottier Bay interim-bulk-storage licence contained a standard "odour nuisance" clause (triggered by five-plus complaints from unrelated residents), but no public record shows those complaints were substantiated. The pre-Miller Clean Environment Commission siting hearings (1987 and 1992) for the Montcalm facility were standard public consultation, not controversy. The closest adverse item is a client-site stop-work order in the Steinbach fire-recovery project — applied to project-site conditions, not a public enforcement proceeding against the company, and resolved via a revised abatement plan. Caveat: absence of evidence in accessible searches is not a legal clean bill of health.

## Financials (what is publicly known)

MEC is private and publishes no revenue, EBITDA, fundraising, or public-equity figures. The only hard data are indirect public-side disclosures from Manitoba's audited statements:
- Manitoba's long-term investment in MEC carried at **C$1.509 million** at March 31, 2023.
- Fixed annual rent from Miller to the provincial landholding corporation of **C$90,000** (a 2008 agreement set C$7,500/month; lease renewed through February 28, 2028).
- A 2010 conversion of **C$509,486** in outstanding rent receivable into **1,242,648** additional preferred shares, bringing the public side's preferred holdings to **4,242,648 shares** — evidence of historic financial stress and how the public side increased its preferred position.

Third-party directories (Dun & Bradstreet, ZoomInfo) carry employee-count estimates only (~45–74 FTE per NPRI 2023). No directly attributable Canadian patent or trademark tied to the Manitoba company surfaced in CIPO-oriented searches (which does not prove none exists).

## Recommendations

**Prospective clients (industrial generators / municipalities):** MEC is effectively the default Manitoba operator with the broadest service envelope in the province. Verify scope coverage of your specific waste codes against Licence 58 HW S2 RRRRR (on gov.mb.ca), ask about the new solvent-recovery pathway if your stream contains contaminated solvents (better recovery profile than cement-kiln fuel-blending), and request MEC's current CHWMEG audit profile.

**Prospective employees:** MEC hires in technical, sales, and finance roles in Winnipeg. Salary + benefits + the $1,000/year post-secondary award and an ISO-45001-aligned safety culture compare favourably to the industry. Strongest fit for chemists, engineers-in-training, vacuum-truck operators, and Class 1A drivers.

**Investors / M&A analysts:** The 50/50 Crown-private structure is durable but means MEC is not a freely tradable asset; investible exposure sits at the Miller Group / White Owl Group parent level (Colas Canada is the corporate parent via the 2018 transaction). Signals to watch: (a) any change to the MHWMC Act altering the Crown stake; (b) commissioning of the solvent-recovery system and its margin uplift; (c) further licence modernizations (a Processing Cell 6 would be a key tell); (d) provincial policy enforcing stricter in-province disposal, which would expand MEC's addressable market.

**Manitoba community stakeholders:** The Citizens Liaison Committee and public registry (file 3440.20) make filings accessible. The 2024–25 solvent-recovery installation is a positive sustainability move, but new on-site chemistry should be tracked. The absence of reported orders or complaints is a meaningful baseline; any future change would be a noteworthy signal.

**Thresholds that should change the above:** a material environmental incident at VBEC, a Director's enforcement order, or substantiated odour complaints under Licence 58 HW S2 RRRRR → re-evaluate counter-party and reputational risk. A change to the 50/50 ownership → reset on the structural-moat thesis. Loss or non-renewal of any ISO certification → re-evaluate as a vendor for ISO-mandated clients.
