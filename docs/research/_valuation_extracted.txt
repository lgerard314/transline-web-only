 White Owl Miller Operating Companies Valuation Scope and method This report estimates the current enterprise value and equity value of the White Owl-owned, Miller-branded waste and environmental operating businesses in Canada , using the scope assumptions you specified. I therefore treat the valuation perimeter as the operating businesses publicly presented under the Miller brand—principally Miller Waste Systems, Miller Compost, and Miller Environmental —and I exclude White Owl real estate unless clearly owned by an operating company . I also exclude the Escarpment Renewables, Maryland Farms, and related Pickering pre-processing assets , because Walker publicly said it acquired those Miller-operated assets in March 2026. Miller’s own current website presents Miller Waste Systems, Miller Compost, and Miller Environmental as its commercial operating lines, while Walker’s acquisition announcement identifies the sold Grimsby, Maryland, and Pickering assets separately. [1] The central challenge is that I did not locate public opco financial statements disclosing Miller revenue, EBITDA, or debt . Accordingly, the valuation below is an estimation exercise , built from public operating scale markers, municipal contract disclosures, official press releases, public trading multiples, and disclosed Canadian environmental/waste transactions. Where data are unavailable, I state them as unspecified and use bounded ranges. All scenario values below are in C$ millions unless noted otherwise . Public-company market values are current as of June 3, 2026 , while most operating and EBITDA data are from the companies’ latest reported FY2025 results. Executive summary My best current estimate for the White Owl-owned Miller-branded operating companies is a base-case enterprise value of approximately C$787 million and a base-case equity value of approximately C$576 million , with a reasonable current range of about C$418 million to C$1.23 billion of EV and C$255 million to C$994 million of equity value . The base case assumes approximately C$650 million of revenue , C$84.7 million of EBITDA , a blended EV/EBITDA multiple of 9.3x , and net debt of 2.5x EBITDA . Those assumptions reflect: substantial operating scale; sticky municipal exposure; a meaningful but subscale hazardous-waste business; and a discount to landfill-owning public peers because Miller explicitly says it does not own or operate landfills , while the highest-value biogas/RNG assets were sold to Walker in 2026. [2] The most important reason the valuation is not higher is that Miller’s public profile suggests a business mix weighted toward collection, transfer, recycling, organics processing, and hazardous waste , but without landfill ownership . Public landfill owners and integrated North American platforms such as WM, Republic, Waste Connections, and Casella trade around the mid-teens on EV/EBITDA, while GFL—after major portfolio reshaping—screens nearer 10x. Miller therefore deserves a discount to the landfill-heavy public set, but likely not a distressed one given its scale, municipal contract density, and long operating history. [3] The most important reason the valuation is not lower is that the public operating evidence is substantial. Official and quasi-official sources point to a platform that, at different times, has been described as serving more than 50 municipalities , operating 35 to 45 sites , employing 1,800 to 3,000 people , running 800 to roughly 1,900 trucks , and serving more than 10,000 commercial clients . In January 2024 Ottawa alone awarded Miller a C$491.7 million initial seven-year curbside collection contract beginning in 2026, while Halton’s disclosed one-year extension economics imply nearly C$28 million of annualized non-blue-box revenue for the extension period. Even before counting most other municipalities, commercial customers, transfer operations, compost, recycling, and hazardous waste, the public contract and operating-scale floor is already material. [15] A second check is the employee-based revenue lens . Casella reported C$1.837 billion-equivalent revenue? No—Casella reported US$1.837 billion of revenue and said it had more than 5,600 team members , which implies approximately US$328,000 of revenue per employee . My Miller scenarios imply roughly C$167,000 to C$267,000 of revenue per employee using the 3,000-employee AMCS figure, which is intentionally below Casella’s level and directionally sensible for a business that appears less landfill-rich and more contracted-hauling/processing oriented. [16] Valuation assumptions Because Miller’s actual revenue is not publicly disclosed in the sources reviewed, I use three revenue anchors together. First, the contract floor above shows that a meaningful portion of revenue is already visible. Second, the employee and truck counts suggest a platform much larger than a local or sub-regional operator. Third, the company’s own and partner materials indicate a mix of municipal , commercial , transfer , recycling , organics , and hazardous waste activities across multiple provinces. On that basis, I set total revenue scenarios at C$500 million , C$650 million , and C$800 million . Those translate into approximately C$263,000 , C$342,000 , and C$421,000 of revenue per truck-equivalent using the AMCS fleet count of 1,900, and approximately C$167,000 , C$217,000 , and C$267,000 of revenue per employee using the 3,000-employee count. [17] I split the business into two valuation blocks. The first is core Miller Waste / Compost / retained organics and recycling / transfer / collection , which I treat as the dominant revenue source. The second is Miller Environmental hazardous waste and related environmental services , which I model as the smaller but higher-margin segment. That segmentation is supported by White Owl’s own historic description of Miller Environmental as a hazardous-waste leader in Manitoba, by Miller’s current operating-line presentation, and by public-sector evidence that hazardous-waste and environmental solution segments can carry higher EBITDA margins than ordinary hauling. Republic’s Environmental Solutions segment generated US$372 million of adjusted EBITDA on US$1.766 billion of revenue in 2025, while Clean Harbors reported US$1.17 billion of adjusted EBITDA on US$6.03 billion of revenue for 2025 and US$1.344 billion of segment adjusted EBITDA on US$5.193 billion of Environmental Services revenue . [18] The multiple framework starts from public comp evidence and then discounts it. WM, Republic, Waste Connections, Casella, and Clean Harbors all screen around the mid-teens EV/EBITDA , while GFL screens materially lower at about 10x based on its current simplified portfolio and lower leverage after major asset monetizations. I then discount Miller’s core business to 7.5x to 10.0x because Miller says it does not own landfills , the 2026 Walker sale removed the more obvious specialty biogas/RNG valuation premium, and there is no public EBITDA disclosure. For Miller Environmental, I use 9.0x to 12.5x , still below or around public hazardous/environmental benchmarks. As a Canadian M&A anchor, GFL’s announced Terrapure purchase was C$927.5 million for a target with C$375 million of annualized revenue and C$110 million of adjusted EBITDA including C$12.5 million of synergies , implying about 8.4x EBITDA including synergies and roughly 2.5x revenue . That transaction is an important floor for a scaled Canadian environmental platform. [19] Net debt is the least observable item. I found no public disclosure of Miller opco debt , lease debt, equipment financing, internal shareholder loans, or post-closure liabilities. I therefore treat net debt as unspecified and model it at 3.0x EBITDA in the conservative case, 2.5x EBITDA in the base case, and 2.0x EBITDA in the aggressive case. Those assumptions are not heroic: Waste Connections reported 2.75x year-end debt-to-EBITDA , Republic reported approximately 2.6x total debt-to-EBITDA , WM’s year-end debt and cash imply about 3.0x net debt-to-EBITDA , GFL reported 3.4x net leverage , and Casella reported a 2.34x consolidated net leverage ratio .
